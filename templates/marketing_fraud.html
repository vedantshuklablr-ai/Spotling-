üéØ Types of Deceptive Marketing Fraud

Recognize these dangerous marketing tactics to avoid becoming a victim

üö´ False Health & Medical Claims

Critical Risk

What it is: Marketing products as cures for serious illnesses without scientific evidence. Common in supplements, weight loss, and diabetes products.

Red Flags:
  • "Cure" claims for diseases (cancer, diabetes, etc.)
  • "100% guaranteed" results
  • Fake doctor endorsements or testimonials
  • Before/after photos that are heavily edited
  • Claims banned by regulatory bodies
üî¥ Examples: "Miracle weight loss pills - lose 10kg in 1 week!", "Cure diabetes permanently", "Cancer treatment without side effects"
‚úì How to Verify: Check AYUSH/FDA approvals, consult real doctors, look for scientific studies, and verify testimonials independently.

üíä Fake Medicine & Supplements

Critical Risk

What it is: Counterfeit or substandard medicines and supplements being sold as original products at cheap prices.

Red Flags:
  • Prices significantly below market rate (usually >50% cheaper)
  • Poor quality packaging or printing
  • No hologram, batch number, or expiry date
  • Sold from suspicious online stores
  • No prescription required for prescription drugs
‚úì How to Identify Fake Medicines: Buy from licensed pharmacies, verify hologram authenticity, check batch numbers on official websites, and report counterfeits to FDA/DGFT.

üíÑ Misleading Beauty & Cosmetics Ads

Critical Risk

What it is: Beauty products marketed with false claims about skin transformation, results, or ingredients.

Red Flags:
  • Extreme skin transformation claims
  • "Removes wrinkles in 7 days" type guarantees
  • Heavily filtered or AI-generated before/after images
  • Celebrity endorsements (often paid and controlled)
  • Claims about "proven" results with no evidence
‚úì How to Protect: Research ingredients independently, check dermatologist reviews, look for clinical studies, and avoid extreme transformation claims.

üíº Fake Job/Income Schemes

Critical Risk

What it is: "Work from home" opportunities and income schemes promising unrealistic earnings for minimal work.

Red Flags:
  • "Earn ‚Çπ50,000/month from home working 1 hour/day"
  • Requires upfront investment or buying inventory
  • MLM (Multi-Level Marketing) scheme structures
  • No clear job description or employer
  • Pressure to recruit others
‚úì How to Verify: Research the company on official databases, ask detailed questions about work, request everything in writing, and avoid upfront payments.

üõçÔ∏è Misleading E-commerce Ads

High Risk

What it is: Deceptive product listings with fake discounts, manipulated images, or misleading product specifications.

Red Flags:
  • Unrealistic discount percentages (90% off)
  • Product images don't match actual items
  • Dimensions or specifications that don't add up
  • Fake customer reviews with same language
  • Too good to be true prices on luxury items
‚úì How to Protect: Read detailed specifications, check actual customer reviews, compare prices across platforms, and order only from verified sellers.

üè¶ Fake Investment & Cryptocurrency Schemes

High Risk

What it is: Guaranteed high returns schemes, pyramid schemes, or fake cryptocurrency investments promising unrealistic profits.

Red Flags:
  • "Guaranteed returns of 20-50% monthly"
  • "Get rich quick" schemes
  • Bitcoin/crypto schemes that seem too good to be true
  • Pressure to invest and recruit friends
  • No regulatory registration or license
‚úì How to Protect: Check SEBI/RBI registration, understand the investment fully, be wary of guaranteed returns, and invest only with regulated entities.

üöó Deceptive Auto & Real Estate Ads

High Risk

What it is: Manipulated vehicle or property images, hidden defects, or false specifications in listings.

Red Flags:
  • Car photos heavily edited or from different angles
  • Property images vs. reality don't match
  • Hidden defects or damage not disclosed
  • Pressure to buy without inspection
  • Payments requested before viewing
‚úì How to Protect: Inspect in person, ask for unedited photos, get written documentation, use third-party inspection services, and involve legal advice.

üëï Fake Designer/Branded Products

Medium Risk

What it is: Counterfeit copy of branded products sold as original at suspiciously low prices.

Red Flags:
  • Prices 80% below original retail
  • Poor quality packaging
  • Spelling errors on logos/labels
  • Sellers unwilling to provide authenticity certificates
  • Sourced from unauthorized channels
‚úì How to Verify: Buy from official websites/authorized dealers, check authenticity codes, examine quality closely, and ask for authenticity certificates.

üìö Fake Educational Programs

Medium Risk

What it is: Misleading online courses, certifications, or training programs with false credentials or job guarantees.

Red Flags:
  • "Get IIT certified" from unrecognized platforms
  • "100% job guarantee after course"
  • Courses costing ‚Çπ10,000+ for basic content
  • No proper course curriculum or syllabus
  • Pressure to pay before course start
‚úì How to Verify: Check platform accreditation, verify instructor credentials, read actual student reviews, and check if certificates are recognized.

ü§≥ Influencer Fraud & Fake Endorsements

Social Risk
  • Bot Followers: Influencers with fake followers/engagement but claiming authentic reach
  • Undisclosed Sponsorships: Paid promotions not marked as advertisements
  • Deepfake Endorsements: Celebrities appear to endorse products they never did
  • Fake Reviews: Paid reviewers pretending to be real customers
  • ‚úì How to Check: Use tools like HypeAuditor, check comment quality, verify influencer partnerships officially, and look for #ad or #sponsored tags

üì¢ Viral Content Scams

Engagement Risk
  • Engagement Bait: "Like if you agree", "Share for free ___" posts
  • Fake Giveaways: "Win iPhone - just tag 3 friends and share"
  • Emotional Manipulation: Tragic stories designed to go viral and drive clicks
  • Misleading Headlines: Clickbait that leads to malware or scams
  • ‚úì How to Protect: Don't click suspicious links, verify giveaways on official pages, check sources, and report fake giveaways

üì¢ How to Report Marketing Fraud

Follow these steps to take action against deceptive ads

1

Gather Evidence

Take screenshots of the fraudulent ad/claim with timestamp, URL, seller information, and any communication.

2

Report to Platform

Report the content on the platform (Facebook, Instagram, Google, Amazon, Flipkart, etc.). Use their report or flag options.

3

File Complaint with Authorities

Report to Central Consumer Protection Authority (CCPA), State Consumer Commission, or Cybercrime cell.

4

Contact Relevant Bodies

FDA/AYUSH (medicines), ASCI (advertising), SEBI (investments), RBI (financial products)

5

Document Everything

Keep all communications, receipts, and complaint confirmations for future reference or legal action.
